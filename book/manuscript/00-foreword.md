# Foreword — Preserving Intent

Software projects rarely fail because teams cannot produce code.

They fail because the software that is delivered is not the software that was intended.

Between an idea and a deployment lies a chain of translations. Someone expresses a need. Someone else interprets it. A team discusses it. A model is drawn. A specification is written. A feature is implemented. A test is automated. A pipeline deploys the result. Users encounter the system in production.

At each step, something can be lost.

The original intent may be simplified, misunderstood, over-specified, under-specified, optimized away, distorted by technology choices, or silently changed by assumptions that were never made explicit.

This book calls that loss **specification drift**.

Specification drift is not merely a requirements problem. It is a cognitive, social, technical, and operational problem. It appears wherever human intent is transformed into system behavior.

Many of the most influential practices in modern software development can be understood as attempts to reduce this drift.

Domain-Driven Design gives teams language and models for shared domain understanding.

Behavior-Driven Development gives teams examples and scenarios for expressing expected behavior.

Testing gives teams evidence that implementation still matches intent.

DevOps reduces the gap between development and operation.

Observability makes production behavior visible.

AI parity testing helps teams compare probabilistic systems against trusted expectations, baselines, and prior behavior.

Agile V, as proposed in this book, is a lightweight cognitive work model for organizing these practices around collaboration, verification, and feature-by-feature delivery.

The purpose of this book is not to introduce another heavy methodology.

It is to offer a way of seeing.

Software development is not primarily the act of writing code. It is the act of preserving intent while transforming ideas into operational reality.

Intentional Development is the discipline of making that transformation visible, collaborative, and verifiable.
